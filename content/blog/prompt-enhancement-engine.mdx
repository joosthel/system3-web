There's a surprisingly tedious problem in AI visual production that doesn't get much attention: writing the prompts.

Not model quality, not compute costs, not hallucinated fingers. Those are real issues, but they're obvious. The one I kept running into was more mundane — translating "this is what the scene should feel like" into structured prompts that actually work, then doing it a dozen times across different tools while keeping the visual language consistent.

Every shot from scratch. Every time.

## What a good prompt actually is

A good prompt isn't a description. It's a compressed art direction brief — lighting logic, texture register, camera behaviour, the mood grammar of a specific project. Writing one well takes craft. Writing twelve of them consistently for the same campaign, each adapted to a different scene and tool? That's just repetitive work. And repetitive work should be automated.

Experienced art directors don't approach each shot from scratch either. They build a visual framework first — a set of decisions about how the world looks, feels, and moves — then apply it to every individual shot. That's what makes a campaign feel coherent rather than just a folder of images.

AI tools don't do this. They're stateless. They don't remember the last shot. Consistency is entirely your problem.

## The tool

I called it the [Prompt Enhancement Engine](https://promptenhancer.joosthelfers.com), which is a serious name for something I built because I was annoyed.

You give it a creative brief and some reference images. It returns an optimised art direction brief — the visual framework layer — plus a full set of prompts for image generation, image editing, and video creation. All derived from that framework and consistent with each other. Change the brief, everything updates.

The key is the art direction step in the middle. Before writing a single tool-specific prompt, it interprets the brief the way an art director would: defines the lighting language, material and texture logic, colour palette and mood. The prompts are generated from that framework. It's just the right order of operations.

## Stack

Deliberately lean:

- **Framework:** Next.js App Router, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS (all hand-rolled, no component libraries)
- **LLM routing:** OpenRouter — Gemini 2.5 Flash for vision/reference analysis, DeepSeek v3.2 for briefs and prompt generation
- **Validation:** Zod for all structured LLM output at runtime
- **Deployment:** Vercel with SSE streaming
- **State:** No database, no auth, no state management library. useState and useRef. I wanted to see how minimal I could make it while it's still genuinely useful.

## What's next

The tool keeps a human in the loop, and that's the right call for now. AI generation is still expensive enough per iteration that you want real judgment before committing to a direction.

But what I built is essentially a proof of concept for something broader: agentic art direction. Brief interpretation, visual trend research, pulling current references, iterative prompt refinement based on output evaluation — all of this is automatable. Not to remove the human, but to move them upstream: into creative strategy, brand judgment, and final approval, rather than the mechanical translation between a good idea and a good output.

The teams producing the best AI content won't be the ones with the sharpest prompts. They'll be the ones who build the best systems around prompting.
